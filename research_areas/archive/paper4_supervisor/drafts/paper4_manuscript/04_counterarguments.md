## 5 Counterarguments and Boundaries

### The Chandler Convergence Is Structural, Not Anthropomorphic

The Chandlerian analogy is not a metaphor imported for style. It is a convergence claim about structure. Human firms and recursive AI systems both encounter governance failure when generation and evaluation are co-located under optimization pressure. The common solution is separation of roles, not because firms and models are secretly the same thing, but because the underlying control problem has the same shape. The analogy should therefore be used where it is load-bearing and stopped where it is not. Agents in this system are not Chandlerian divisions with independent sovereignty.

### The Bitter Lesson Does Not Solve Governance

The Bitter Lesson is a claim about representation learning, not a governance theorem. Better learned representations can improve capability and still worsen camouflage. A stronger model can become better at manufacturing compliance theater. So the relevant distinction is not hand-engineering versus learning. It is whether the enforcement floor is inside the optimizing loop or outside it.

### Principal Scale and Scope Boundaries

The paper should stay honest about scale. The current evidence is for a single principal supervising one recursive system. The architecture may generalize; the evidence does not yet prove multi-principal or large-firm operation. That boundary is a strength if stated clearly and a weakness if blurred.

### Complexity Ceiling

The M-Form imposes overhead. That is a real tradeoff, not a rhetorical concession. The paper's argument is that effective capability is raw capability discounted by self-deception and rework. A system with higher nominal autonomy but weak governance can underperform a more constrained system once agency cost is priced in. The manuscript should state this as a tradeoff, not as a claim that governance is free.
